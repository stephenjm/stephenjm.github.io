Career Break — Reading Journal

I didn't take time off to rest.
I took time off to think.

For years I was executing well but asking fewer and fewer of the questions that matter. These six books — read alongside building a Certificate Authority, a Key Management Service, and a cryptographic library — were my attempt to fix that.

Stephen April 2026 6 books · 3 repositories · still going

TL;DR — What this is really about

"Stay hungry. Stay foolish."

— Steve Jobs, Stanford Commencement Address, 2005

I used to read that quote as a call to ambition. I now read it as a discipline — the deliberate choice to remain uncomfortable, to keep asking the questions that do not have clean answers yet, to resist the pull toward expertise as a resting state.

This page is not a retrospective. It is a snapshot mid-climb. The Certificate Authority, the KMS, the crypto-client — these are not portfolio pieces. They are the proof of work behind questions I am still sitting with:

  • When AI agents make thousands of small decisions per second inside a distributed system, and humans defer the majority of that work, what happens to ACID guarantees? Who owns the transaction boundary when the agent does not know it crossed one?
  • Teams building AI-assisted systems are routinely intermixing ACID and BASE patterns without realising it — because the agent does not distinguish between them. For security-sensitive projects, that gap is not a performance trade-off. It is a CIA triad failure waiting to happen.
  • CAP theorem does not disappear because the code was generated quickly. If anything, the risk of violating consistency guarantees increases when no single human holds the full design in their head — and the agent never did to begin with.
  • Where does the C4 model break down when the component is an LLM with probabilistic outputs and no stable interface contract? And what does staff-level engineering judgment look like when the most junior member of the team never sleeps, never asks for clarification, and never flags that it is guessing?
"AI didn't make me faster at building. It made me faster at everything except the parts that actually matter."

That realisation landed mid-build — around the time I was designing the PKI trust hierarchy and noticed that every suggestion Copilot made was syntactically plausible and architecturally naive. It would scaffold a certificate signing flow without once asking whether the serial number was RFC 5280 compliant, or whether the backend could handle revocation at scale. Fast. Confidently wrong.

The books did not teach me to distrust the tools. They taught me which decisions the tools cannot own — and why that boundary matters more now than it ever did.

The Reading List

01
Engineering Leadership

The Staff Engineer's Path

Tanya Reilly

I thought I understood what staff-level engineering meant. I had been operating at that level for years. Reilly's book made me realise I had been describing the symptoms — broad ownership, influence without authority, technical direction — without ever naming the underlying shift: that judgment, not output, becomes your primary deliverable.

The uncomfortable moment: she describes engineers who are technically excellent but structurally invisible — high output, low leverage. Delivery discipline without strategic intent is just fast execution in the wrong direction. That pattern is easier to recognise in a team than in yourself.

Applied as

The tiered AI autonomy model — more human control at higher C4 levels, more agent latitude at lower ones — is Reilly's calibrated delegation model, applied to tooling rather than people. The agent is not a junior engineer. It is a tool that requires the same calibrated oversight.

02
Architecture Foundations

Fundamentals of Software Architecture

Mark Richards and Neal Ford

The book's central argument is uncomfortable: most architects optimise for the decisions they prefer to make and avoid the ones that are genuinely hard. Every choice is a trade-off, and failing to name the trade-off is not neutrality — it is a decision made badly.

The real risk in AI-assisted architecture is not that the agent makes a wrong choice. It is that it makes a choice at all at the wrong level — generating Container-level assumptions embedded silently inside Component-level code, with no ARCHITECTURE.md entry, no rationale, and no owner. The trade-off was made. Nobody signed it.

Applied as

Every Context and Container decision in the CA project has an explicit ARCHITECTURE.md entry naming the trade-off — not just the decision. Self-hosted PKI over managed CA: operational control versus operational burden, documented and owned by a human, not generated.

03
Data and Distributed Systems

Designing Data-Intensive Applications

Martin Kleppmann

Kleppmann connects ideas that are usually taught in isolation: replication, consistency, consensus, stream processing. The mental model that emerges holds together under pressure in a way that individual technique knowledge does not.

The friction point was this: AI agents do not have a consistency model. They generate code that works locally and may silently intermix ACID and BASE patterns across service boundaries — because they are optimising for syntactic correctness, not transactional integrity. In a security-sensitive system, that is not a performance trade-off. It is a potential CIA triad failure: data that should be confidential flowing through an eventually-consistent path with no integrity guarantee and no audit trail. CAP theorem does not disappear because the code was generated quickly.

Applied as

The AuditLog in the CA is append-only, separately signed, and tamper-evident — a deliberate consistency guarantee, not a default. Envelope encryption in the KMS enforces a clear data boundary. These were human decisions, made explicit because an agent would not have made them at all.

04
Component Design

Software Architecture: The Hard Parts

Neal Ford, Mark Richards, Pramod Sadalage and Zhamak Dehghani

This book is structured as a series of architectural arguments with no clean resolution — which is either frustrating or honest depending on how long you have been in the industry. The hard parts of architecture are hard because the trade-offs are real, not because the engineers involved were insufficiently clever.

The granularity chapter surfaced a personal pattern: a tendency to over-decompose — to fragment systems into components that feel clean but introduce coupling at the integration layer that is harder to reason about than the thing being escaped. With AI agents in the loop, that risk compounds. The agent will happily generate a microservice boundary that violates the component contract you designed two sprints ago, because it does not hold the full system context. The coupling is invisible until it is not.

Applied as

The KmsProvider interface — human-designed at component level — and the four adapter implementations — AI-assisted at code level — held precisely because the boundary was named and constrained before the agent touched it. The .github/instructions/ file encoded the contract. The agent operated inside it.

05
Visualising Architecture

The C4 Model for Visualising Software Architecture

Simon Brown

Brown's model is almost too simple to take seriously until you try to apply it under pressure. Four levels — Context, Container, Component, Code — each answering a different question at a different altitude. The discipline is not the diagrams. It is the habit of asking: which level am I operating at right now, and am I answering the right question for this altitude?

The confusion I recognised was between Component and Container decisions — making code-level choices that encoded container-level assumptions, then finding the system harder to change than it should have been. AI agents make this worse, not better. They operate exclusively at the code level but their outputs carry implicit container assumptions the human never reviewed. Brown makes that confusion visible. Once you see it, you see it everywhere.

Applied as

The four-level AI autonomy matrix uses C4 levels as its primary axis. Context: human only. Code: fully agentic with constrained instructions. The model works because Brown's altitude distinction is precise enough to act on — and specific enough to encode into .github/instructions/ files.

06
Systems Thinking

Thinking in Systems

Donella H. Meadows

Meadows is not writing about software. She is writing about systems as stocks, flows, and feedback loops — and once you internalise that framing, you cannot look at a codebase the same way. A CI pipeline is a feedback loop. A growing bug backlog is a reinforcing loop. An architectural decision that makes future change harder is a delayed-feedback trap.

The hardest chapter was on leverage points. Meadows argues that interventions almost always happen at the wrong place: optimising flows rather than changing the structure that governs them. Applied to AI-assisted development: adding more agents, faster CI, or better prompts are flow optimisations. They do not change the underlying feedback structure. If the architecture has no verification gate between AI generation and production deployment, faster generation means faster compounding of unreviewed risk — not faster delivery of safe software.

Applied as

The .github/instructions/ files are a Meadows stock — a constraint that shapes agent behaviour before generation begins. The Jidoka verification gates are feedback loops closed at source. The intervention is structural, not flow-level. That distinction is the whole point.
